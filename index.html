Strand

A survival rougelike about fending off zombies.

Time Span: 2022-2024

Team Size: 9 person team for 2022, solo team for 2023-2024

Strand was initially created as a club project of a game development club I founded. Initially, the project was worked on during club meeting hours by a group of about 9 people. However, I continued to work on it individually after my graduation. During this project's time as a club project, I was project lead and directed art and music. Most of my work in this project is in programming/art.

This project was made in C# Unity, with Github as the source control. The main gameplay loop of this game involves traveling from the center into a randomly generated map to collect scrap. The player character throws and swings items to fend off various types of zombies.

An example of a generated map

I programmed the randomly generating map that Strand takes place in. The map is done in a room based system. The entire map is a grid of rooms with openings to other rooms. To determine openings in a map, each room randomly opens some of its sides. Afterwords, the entire map is processed so that every room can be reached with the given openings. "Dead ends" in the map are assigned to have a function of having loot, either in the form of food or scrap.

Each room in the map is assigned a difficulty number, function, and open directions. In order to fill each room with a layout corresponding to the opening, function, and difficulty values, I made a text proccessor that takes values from a .txt file and turns them into layouts. Afterwards, I could randomly assign one of the valid layouts to each room, completing the overall layout of the entire map.

Some text from the custom grid editor

Display of all enemies within the game

One other major component of the game I programmed lies in enemy design and development. All of the enemies have a base class, as most of them have finite hp, the ability to be grabbed, and a stun state. Within the main state, many empty virtual functions were made such as a effect on being grabbed and an effect on being stunned. This collectively makes the process of creating unique enemies much simpler.

Each unique type of enemy usually has the pattern of staying at one spot until the player comes into view/enters the same tile as the enemy. They then all will have unique movement patterns in trying to get to or damage the player.

Display of all weapons within the game

Weapons in the game are divided into golden scrap and normal scrap. Golden scrap have unusual properties like allowing for telportation or shooting projectiles while base scrap are just copies of eachother with modified number parameters *e.g. one basic weapon stuns a bit longer while another does more damage*.

I made a sizeable chunk of the art in this game us the pixel editor Piskel. Menu's, half of enemy animations, and tile sprites were made by me. With this specific project I was aiming to provide a more "complete" game than most of the games I made earlier. Because of that, I did spend a sizeable amount of time trying to visually polish the game.

Frogology

An Animal Conservationist Simulator.

Time Span: 5 week gamejam in 2024

Team Size: 8 people

Trailer created by teammate Micah Ballard

Frogology was made for the "IndieCade Summer Game Design Internship" in which students participated in the 2024 Indiecade Climate Jam. Along with the game jam, the internship also required students to complete other tasks such as drafting plans on how to actually impact enviormental pursuits throug the game.

Internship Certification Letter

The game was programmed in the Unity game engine with me and two other programmers. The game was made in C# and we worked together using Github as version control. The main gameplay loop of the game involved learning more about nearby animals while catching them.

Credits of Frogology

Demonstration of out of view animal AI

One of the main gameplay elements I was in charge of was programming and designing the behavior of the animals. Animal behavior could be divided up into two states, present and out of view. When animals move out of view all of their physics are disabled and they move between objects with certain preset tags. When animals move within range of a player, they materialize in a nearby bush and begin wandering around.

Present animals generally either wander in a random direction or run towards/away from predator/prey. Animals are able to avoid collisions while moving by shapecasting in several directions until it finds a direction in which it does not hit an object. Animal sight detection is triggered when a object is a set angle away from the foward vector. Animals are able to hear by scanning objects with certain tags and checking their distance from said animal.

Demonstration of present animal AI

Editor representation of overarching quest flow

Another important gameplay element I worked on was the overarching branching quest system of the game. I created a quest creation editor tool that shows connections between quests and the contents of quests that are created through inspector and a editor tool for branching dialogue based on the current quest.

The quests document most if not all of the progression in the game, dialogue is locked behind quests, the ability to catch certain animals is locked between quests, and the ability to sleep in the game is locked behind quests. I handled locking events and abilities behind quests and events where quests would increment or complete. A save system for quest progress and species logging process was also implemented through playerprefs.

Head Hunters

A funk themed turn based strategy game.

Time Span: 2021-2022

Team Size: Solo Project

Headhunter's is a game I started and finished in highschool. It was made in Unity C# and Github is used as the source control. The game is a strategy game where the player controls three characters to fight against bosses. A total of 6 bosses were created.

The player can command each character to either shoot, use a special move, or move to a different tile and the opposing bosses can do the same.

The turn system that this game is based around, is a que of events that get played on the player fully deciding what to do. While this allows players and enemies to perform their actions, this also allows for other interactions like queing status effects and displaying special messages. Players essentially interacted with the game by queing in events that would be preformed by player characters.

Both enemies and players have access to special moves, a catagory of moves with unique effects ranging from reducing the speed of enemies to rigging another character to explode in a set amount of turns. The when the player chooses any special move, tiles that these special moves can be cast on are highlighted, and the resulting effect area are also highlighted.

The move selection screen

These enemies moves and attacks exactly like a knight in chess

The enemies in this game have all of the same abilities that a player character has like, shooting, moving, or using a special move. Thus, a system is needed to handle enemy behavior. It uses system that adjusts the priority based on the path to a location on the map and the potential final action that the enemy takes. At the end, an action is randomly selected from pool of actions with the most priority.

The enemies also consider the properties of their special moves while pathfinding, they pathfind with movement abilities and find tiles in which they are in range for offensive abilities. Ultimately with this system, modifying priority paramaters and special moves allowed me to make enemies to display a wide range of unique behaviors.

Enemy boss uses movement ability to move farther away from player characters

Since this was a solo project, I ended up making pretty much all of the art/music assets by myself. All of the animations are done through Unity's animation system and the illustrations themselves were done through google draw.

The music was done through Musescore and leaned very heavily into funk and rock music. The game itself is very inspired music from those generes too. The name of the game itself is taken from the album "Head Hunters" by Herbie Hancock. The boss "Face Gatherers" had enemies with named by the songs in the same album. The enemy Heavenmaker had moves with the same name as multiple song titles of the math rock band Elephant Gym.

Velvet

An action platformer revolving around hookshots.

Time Span: 2019-2020

Team Size: Solo Project

This game was made in Unity C#. The main gameplay loop is that the player dodges bullets while shooting and meleeing enemies. The hookshot is simply a tag that pulls the players towards it, and enemies attack by firing at the player at an angle that is offset by a random amount.

A total of 11 levels were made and designed along with three enemy types.

Riptide Gardens

A farming game with a constantly shifting base.

Time Span: 1 week in 2024

Team Size: 3 people

This was originally part of the "Spring 2D Jam '24" but the concept the team created ended up requiring more time. The game was made with Unity C# with Github as the source control. The game worked off a grid system where plants would get boosts and debuffs depending on what kind of plants they were next to and how fertile the soil was or not. Waves would shift the landscape of the island periodically, destroying plants and shifting fertility. Fertility is mostly determined by proximity to sea.

10 different plants were created all with different properties. Some plants are direct upgrades of other ones, some plants do better in fertile/infertile soil, some plants boost other plants, and some plants sabotage other plants. However, they all have the same base properties of producing food at set times and not being able to be placed in water.

Checking In

A starical hopsital clerk simulator.

Time Span: 2 days in 2023

Team Size: 6 people

This was made for Ludum Dare 54 and made to comply to the theme "Limited Space".The game was made with Unity C# with Github as the source control. The game has the player decide to either admit, waitlist, or reject a que of patients. Players are given the list of avalable rooms, the waitlist size, the insurance of the patient, and the injury of the patient.

I worked with patient data made by a teammate to create a patient generator that produces patients with unique chances of death, payout, and treatment time, I created the room vacancy and waitlist system that determines if a patient is able to be admitted, and I polished up a notification system made by another programmer.
